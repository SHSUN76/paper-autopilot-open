# Paper Writing Style Guide

> Adapted from an experienced PI's paper-writing guide. A field-tested style and
> formatting reference for preparing high-quality manuscripts (battery / materials
> science conventions, but broadly applicable). Journal templates override any rule
> here — always follow the target journal's author guidelines.

---

## 1. General Approach

- Aim to publish in well-respected journals. Publishing in good venues is hard: read
  widely, understand the state of the field, think deeply, design thoughtful experiments,
  obtain reliable data, and analyze it logically. Wide reading is not optional — it is the
  foundation of good work and good writing.
- Favor quality over quantity. One well-supported paper in a respected journal is worth
  more than several thin ones.
- Decide the target journal early, and settle authorship early. Co-authors should have
  made a significant experimental or concrete intellectual contribution. Adding names
  without a real contribution is unethical.
- Before writing, read the target journal's instructions and study a few of its recent
  papers to internalize its format, style, and reference conventions. Every journal is
  different — note this carefully.

## 2. Structure & Length

- **Sentence length**: avoid long sentences; they make readers lose the thread.
- **Paragraph length**: no single paragraph should exceed one page when double-spaced.
  Break paragraphs at logical points to aid readability.
- **Conciseness**: present facts and data clearly and without redundancy.
- **Originality**: never copy sentences from published papers — that is plagiarism.
- **First page**: include title, author names, affiliation, abstract, and the
  corresponding author's contact details, unless the journal specifies otherwise.
- **Acknowledgments**: place an acknowledgment section after the conclusion; confirm the
  correct funding sources to credit.
- **References / figure pages**: begin the reference list on a separate page; each figure
  caption on a separate page and each figure on its own page, unless the journal says
  otherwise.
- Do not write identical sentences in the abstract and the conclusion.

## 3. Language & Style

- Writing requires thinking: each sentence needs coherence and connection to the ones
  before and after. Connectives ("however", "in contrast", "on the other hand", etc.)
  used at the right places improve the presentation — but do not overuse filler.
- Use simple, clear language so readers understand easily; avoid needlessly obscure words.
- Avoid contractions: write "it is", not "it's". Apply this throughout.
- Replace "using" with "with" where it reads better (e.g., "carried out with XPS").
- Prefer "the larger size of lithium" over "lithium's larger size" (avoid possessives for
  scientific quantities).
- Keep the US English dictionary/spell-check on for the whole manuscript (or the journal's
  required variant), and apply it consistently.
- Reserve "significant" for statistical significance; use "large" or "important" otherwise.

## 4. Numbers, Notation & Units

- Put a space around operators and before a decimal in expressions: "x = 0.5", not "x=0.5".
- Space an approximation: "~ 15", not "~15".
- Space between a number and its unit: "150 nm".
- Space inequalities: "0 ≤ x ≤ 1" and "x < 1", not "0≤x≤1" / "x<1".
- Space composition ratios: "Mn : Ni = 3 : 1", not "Mn:Ni = 3:1".
- Write compound units with spaces: "mA h g⁻¹" (not "mAh/g"), "m² g⁻¹" (not "m²/g").
  Whichever convention the journal uses, apply it consistently.
- Time and rate: "5 h" (not "5 hours"), "24 h", "40 s". Rate "5C" has **no** space
  between the number and C; e.g., "300 mA h g⁻¹ at 5C rate".
- Avoid unwarranted decimal precision in reported values (e.g., report "321 mA h g⁻¹",
  not "321.7 mA h g⁻¹", unless the precision is justified).
- Do not rely on auto-formatting of numbers; verify them manually.
- Define every abbreviation at its first appearance in the main text (starting in the
  introduction), even if it was already used in the abstract.

## 5. Figures & Captions

- Draw high-quality figures. Use a large font size — figures are often reduced in print, so
  they must remain legible after reduction.
- Use contrasting symbols and/or colors to distinguish data sets within one figure. Use
  good figure-making software.
- In captions, place panel labels — (a), (b), (c) — at the **beginning** of each item, not
  the end. Write "cyclability of (a) LiCoO₂ and (b) LiNiO₂", not
  "cyclability of LiCoO₂ (a) and LiNiO₂ (b)".
- The overall arrangement of text, figures, and tables should look clean and deliberate; a
  careless-looking manuscript invites criticism.

## 6. References & Citations

- **Reference number placement**: the period or comma precedes a superscript reference
  number, **except** for Nature journals. When the number is in square brackets or
  parentheses, the period/comma follows the closing bracket. Examples:
  "it has been studied.¹" or "it has been studied [1]."; for Nature style: "it has been
  studied¹."
- **Do not use auto-numbering** for references (or anywhere) — enter the numbers manually.
- Use a consistent file-naming convention for the manuscript (e.g., journal abbreviation +
  a couple of descriptive words).

## 7. Punctuation & Term Conventions

- Two items: "x and y". Three or more: "x, y, and z" (serial comma). Do not use a comma
  before "and" for a two-item list; do use it for three or more.
- "respectively" goes with the pairing, not dumped at the end:
  "x, y, and z give, respectively, a, b, and c."
- Hyphenate attributive compounds consistently: "lithium-metal anode", not
  "lithium metal anode".
- Do not auto-number subheadings; number them manually if numbering is used.

## 8. Formatting

- Margins and font: 1-inch margins on all sides; Times New Roman 12-point — unless the
  journal template dictates otherwise.
- Indent paragraphs by 0.375 inch (or per the journal template).
- Page numbers at the bottom of each page, centered. Never submit without page numbers.

## 9. Data Integrity

- You are responsible for the accuracy and reproducibility of every data point. If you are
  unsure about a result, do not include it — repeat the experiment to confirm it. Sanity-
  check whether each value makes physical sense.
- Analyze data logically. Identify flaws, design better experiments to strengthen weak
  points, and let the analysis — not assertion — carry the argument.

## 10. Proofreading

- Read the manuscript carefully several times before submission, checking English,
  sentence construction, data accuracy, and the correctness of figure/table numbers.
  Ensure every sentence makes sense and conveys its intended message.
- A manuscript prepared with care makes a good impression; a rough one wastes the time of
  both authors and reviewers and risks rejection.

## 11. Revision & Response to Reviewers

- Take reviewers' comments seriously and revise thoroughly. Indicate all textual revisions
  clearly (e.g., a distinct color or tracked changes); changes inside figures/schemes need
  not be color-marked.
- Prepare a response-to-reviewers document in a clear format: the manuscript ID centered at
  the top, then "Reviewer Comment 1" followed by "Response to Comment 1", and so on. Set
  each comment in italics and each response in normal font.
- A well-prepared revision can be accepted by the editor without another review round.

---

**Read and check everything carefully before submission.**
